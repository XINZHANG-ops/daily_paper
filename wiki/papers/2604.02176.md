## Summary

This paper introduces the **Textual Frequency Law (TFL)**, a novel research direction exploring how textual frequency affects Large Language Model performance. The core finding is straightforward yet powerful: when paraphrases carry the same meaning, high-frequency textual data should be preferred over low-frequency data for both prompting and fine-tuning. This holds because high-frequency expressions presumably appeared more frequently during the model's pre-training, making them easier for the model to process and understand.

The authors address a practical problem: when computational resources for training and prompting are limited, which paraphrases are most useful? Different prompts with identical meanings can yield dramatically different results, and the textual frequency hypothesis explains why. High-frequency expressions like "deserted or running away" are more common in training data than low-frequency alternatives like "forsaking or escape," leading to better model comprehension and task performance.

To implement this, the paper proposes three complementary methods. **Textual Frequency Law (TFL)** selects paraphrases with highest sentence-level frequency using word-level frequency multiplication. **Textual Frequency Distillation (TFD)** enhances frequency estimation by querying LLMs to conduct story completion, further extending sentences in datasets. **Curriculum Textual Frequency Training (CTFT)** fine-tunes LLMs in increasing order of sentence-level frequency. The framework estimates frequency through online resources since many LLMs are closed-source in their training data, then uses a paraphrase input method to transform prompts into more frequent textual expressions.

## Key Contributions

1. **Textual Frequency Law (TFL)**: First formal proposal that high-frequency textual data should be preferred for LLMs in both prompting and fine-tuning scenarios when meaning is preserved.

2. **Textual Frequency Distillation (TFD)**: Novel method to enhance frequency estimation by using LLMs to conduct story completion on target datasets, with the generated corpora used to adjust initial frequency estimates from online resources.

3. **Curriculum Textual Frequency Training (CTFT)**: Fine-tuning approach that arranges training data in increasing order of sentence-level frequency across epochs, yielding substantial improvements.

4. **TextualFrequencyPairedDataset (TFPD)**: Curated dataset with paired high/low frequency paraphrases across math reasoning (738 pairs from GSM8K), machine translation (526 pairs from FLORES-200), commonsense reasoning, and tool calling tasks.

5. **Comprehensive evaluation**: Tested across 100 languages in machine translation, multiple math reasoning datasets, and agentic tool calling with consistent improvements.

## Method

### Textual Frequency Estimation

The sentence-level frequency is computed as a position-unaware multiplication of word-level frequencies:

$$sfreq(x, D) = \left(\prod_{k=1}^{K} wfreq(x_k, D)\right)^{1/K}$$

This geometric mean of token frequencies approximates how "common" the vocabulary in a sentence is. The frequency function `sfreq` is derived from online corpora since direct access to LLM training data is typically unavailable.

### Textual Frequency Distillation

The TFD method addresses the gap between estimated frequency from online resources and actual LLM training data distribution. It queries LLMs to generate story completions on the training data, producing a distilled dataset D'. The final frequency is computed as:

$$F(x) = \alpha F_1(x) + (1 + \zeta_1(F_1(x) = 0)) \beta F_2(x)$$

Where $F_1$ is the initial frequency from online resources and $F_2$ is the frequency from distilled LLM generations. A strengthening factor $\zeta$ increases the effect of distilled frequency when words yield negligible initial frequency.

### Curriculum Textual Frequency Training

CTFT arranges the training data in increasing order of sentence-level frequency for each epoch:

$$sort_{x_n \in T} (F(x_n))$$

This curriculum approach trains on lower-frequency (harder, more diverse) expressions first, then progressively moves to higher-frequency (easier) expressions, combined with TFL-based paraphrase selection during inference.

## Results

### Math Reasoning (GSM8K)

| Model | Low-Frequency | High-Frequency |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| DeepSeek-V3 | 63.55% | 71.54% (+8%) |
| GPT-4o-mini | 60.70% | 68.70% (+8%) |
| LLaMA-3.3-70B-Instruct | 80.49% | 88.75% (+8%) |

High-frequency prompts improved accuracy by approximately 8 percentage points across all tested models.

### Machine Translation (100 Languages, FLORES-200)

On DeepSeek-V3, high-frequency prompts improved BLEU scores in **99 out of 100** language pairs:
- 63 language pairs improved by more than 1 point
- 31 language pairs improved by more than 3 points
- 12 language pairs improved by more than 5 points

On GPT-4o-mini: 95/100 language pairs showed BLEU improvement with high-frequency prompts.

### Fine-tuning with CTFT (Translation)

On Kabuverdianu (kea_Latn) translation task, CTFT with high-frequency data improved BLEU from 3.78 to 4.91 (**+30%**) compared to fine-tuning without curriculum.

### Commonsense Reasoning & Tool Calling

| Model | Task | Low-Frequency | High-Frequency |
|-------|------|--------------|----------------|
| GPT-4o-mini | CommonsenseQA | 0.6747 | 0.6974 |
| DeepSeek-V3 | CommonsenseQA | 0.7043 | 0.7235 |
| Qwen-2.5-7B | Tool Selection | 0.6316 | 0.6667 |

## Limitations & Future Work

1. **Computational cost of TFD**: Story completion to obtain frequency estimation from LLMs introduces additional computational overhead, though this is optional as TFL remains effective with initial frequency estimates alone.

2. **Semantic equivalence assumption**: The law assumes perfect semantic equivalence between paraphrases, though in practice, paraphrasing inevitably introduces subtle meaning shifts.

3. **Sentence frequency as unigram approximation**: The geometric-mean definition ignores word order and inter-token dependencies, which may not fully capture "commonness" for paraphrases with substantially different syntactic structures.

4. **Limited to paraphrase selection**: The paper focuses on selecting among paraphrases with the same meaning, not on generating better paraphrases from scratch.
