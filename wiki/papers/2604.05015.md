# Video-MME-v2: Towards the Next Stage in Benchmarks for Comprehensive Video Understanding

**Published**: 2026-04-05 | **Added**: 2026-04-08 | **arXiv**: [2604.05015](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.05015)

## Summary

Video-MME-v2 addresses a critical discrepancy in video understanding benchmarks: inflated leaderboard scores mask a widening gap between reported model capabilities and real-world performance. The benchmark introduces a progressive tri-level capability hierarchy spanning from basic visual information aggregation through temporal dynamics modeling to complex multimodal reasoning, paired with a group-based evaluation strategy that enforces consistency across related queries and coherence in multi-step reasoning chains.

The dataset comprises 800 videos with 3,200 questions, annotated through a rigorous human pipeline involving 12 annotators, 50 reviewers, and 3,300 human-hours. Key findings reveal a substantial gap between the best model (Gemini-3-Pro at 49.4 Non-Lin Score) and human experts (90.7), with hierarchical bottlenecks where errors in lower capability levels propagate to higher reasoning. Notably, thinking-based reasoning improves performance with subtitles but can degrade it in purely visual settings, exposing models' over-reliance on language priors.

## Key Contributions

- **Progressive Tri-Level Hierarchy**: From information aggregation (Level 1) through temporal dynamics (Level 2) to complex reasoning (Level 3), ensuring holistic evaluation from visual perception to sophisticated comprehension
- **Group-Based Evaluation Strategy**: Consistency-based groups evaluate capability breadth across related questions; coherence-based groups assess reasoning depth through logically structured question chains
- **Non-Linear Scoring**: Quadratic suppression penalizes fragmented correct guesses and rewards consistent performance; first-error truncation for reasoning chains prevents credit for logically unsupported steps
- **Extensive Human Annotation**: 800 videos, 3,200 questions (8 options each), 12 annotators, 50 reviewers, 3,300 human-hours, 5 rounds of quality assurance

## Method

### Progressive Capability Hierarchy

**Level 1: Visual Information Aggregation**
- Visual Recognition: Object/entity identification, attribute recognition, scene understanding, OCR
- Cross-Modal Consistency: Alignment of visual cues with audio semantics (tone-mood synchronization)
- Basic Counting & Calculation: Counting objects, numerical reasoning from visual content

**Level 2: Temporal Dynamics**
- Action & Motion Analysis: Fine-grained action recognition, motion trajectory tracking
- Sequential Ordering: Chronological order of events/object appearances
- Causal Reasoning: Cause-effect relations grounded in video timeline

**Level 3: Complex Reasoning**
- Narrative Understanding: Plot twists, metaphors, non-linear storytelling interpretation
- Social Dynamics: Dyadic interactions, collective behaviors, conversational dominance
- Physical World Reasoning: Counterfactual reasoning, spatial understanding, counterintuitive phenomena

### Group-Based Evaluation

**Consistency-Based Groups**: Test breadth of specific capabilities by varying question aspects (e.g., relative position vs. spatial reconstruction for spatial understanding) and granularity (e.g., object counting vs. motion counting). Ensures the model can apply consistent understanding across different manifestations of the same concept.

**Coherence-Based Groups**: Test depth of reasoning through sequences of temporally and causally related questions. For a complex plot task, questions progressively evaluate: direct visual clues → anomalous details → underlying purpose → final conclusion. This hierarchical verification distinguishes genuine multi-step reasoning from lucky guessing.

### Non-Linear Scoring

**For Consistency Groups**: Score = (N/4)^2 where N is number of correct answers out of 4 related questions. This quadratic suppression penalizes isolated correct predictions while rewarding consistent performance.

**For Coherence Groups**: First-error truncation—only the longest consecutive correct sequence counts. Once an error occurs, later correct answers are ignored. This prevents credit for correct but logically unsupported steps.

### Dataset Construction

**Video Curation**:
- Recency-oriented: Over 80% published in 2025 or later, nearly 40% after October 2025
- Hierarchical taxonomy: Sports & Competition, Lifestyle & Entertainment, Art & Literature, Knowledge & Education (31 fine-grained subcategories)
- Quality control: 84.3% have >10,000 views; 94.4% exceed 1,000 views
- Manual decontamination: Excludes classic films, flagship influencer content

**Question Design**:
- 8-option multiple-choice (12.5% random guessing probability)
- Adversarial distractors: Highly plausible given partial evidence but contradict ground truth
- Progressive word count: Q1-Q4 questions have increasing complexity matching reasoning depth
- Rigorous verification: Frontier models (Gemini-3-Pro) used for cross-check; text-only baseline removes language-prior solvable questions

## Results

| Human Expert | 90.7 | - | 94.9 |

### Key Findings

**Performance Gap**: A 41.3-point gap separates Gemini-3-Pro (49.4) from human experts (90.7) in group-based non-linear scoring, demonstrating that current MLLMs lack the consistency and logical coherence required for real-world video understanding.

**Hierarchical Bottlenecks**: Errors cascade from lower levels—weak visual aggregation undermines temporal modeling, which in turn limits complex reasoning. Improving high-level reasoning requires holistic enhancement of the entire capability stack.

**Thinking Mode Analysis**:
- With subtitles: Thinking improves performance (+3.8/+5.8 for Qwen3.5-122B-Think)
- Without subtitles: Thinking can cause regression (KimiVL-16B drops -3.3/-3.3)
- Models overweight language-based reasoning; over-reliance on language priors

**Robustness Ratio** (Non-Lin Score / Avg Acc):
- Gemini-3-Pro: 75% (strongest consistency)
- Qwen3.5-397B: 70%
- LLaVA-Video-7B: ~40% (weakest consistency)

## Limitations & Future Work

The benchmark focuses on question-answering; extending to open-ended generation tasks remains future work. The non-linear scoring could be refined based on specific downstream application requirements. Further investigation into reducing language prior over-reliance in purely visual settings is needed.
