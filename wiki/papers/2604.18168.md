# Extending One-Step Image Generation from Class Labels to Text via Discriminative Text Representation

**Published**: 2026-04-19 | **Added**: 2026-04-21 | **arXiv**: [2604.18168](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.18168)

## Summary

MeanFlow achieves remarkable one-step class-conditional image generation, but extending it to text-conditioned generation has proven challenging—the semantic density and continuous distribution of text embeddings make velocity field learning fundamentally harder than with discrete class labels. This paper identifies that effective text-conditioned MeanFlow requires text representations with two key properties: discriminability (capturing subtle differences among semantically similar prompts) and disentanglement (preserving distinct semantic components). By leveraging BLIP3o-NEXT's text encoder, which excels at both properties, and adapting MeanFlow with dual temporal embeddings, the paper achieves GenEval 0.90 with only 4 sampling steps—matching the 30-step baseline while outperforming all distilled models. The key insight is that high-quality text representations reduce the semantic discrimination burden under limited denoising steps.

## Key Contributions

- First exploration of extending MeanFlow's one-step generation from class labels to flexible text conditioning
- Identification of discriminability and disentanglement as the two key properties enabling effective text-conditioned MeanFlow
- EMF model achieves GenEval 0.90 with 4 steps (vs 0.91 for 30-step baseline), outperforming all distilled models
- Analysis reveals that text encoder quality determines velocity field quality under few-step settings

## Method

The core insight is that text representations for MeanFlow must exhibit high discriminability and disentanglement. Discriminability ensures the velocity field has the correct direction even under limited denoising steps; disentanglement ensures that semantic components within complex prompts remain separable. The paper evaluates four text encoders (BLIP3o-NEXT, SANA-1.5, CLIP, T5) on both properties and finds BLIP3o-NEXT superior on both dimensions. The EMF adaptation uses dual temporal embeddings: one encoding the interval length (t−r) and another encoding the segment end time (t), combined as φcond(t,r) = φinterval(t−r) + φend(t). Timesteps (t,r) are adaptively sampled from uniform or logit-normal distributions, with the ratio of non-equal timesteps increasing throughout training.

## Results

| Configuration | Steps | GenEval | DPG-Bench | HPS-v2 |
|--------------|-------|---------|-----------|--------|
| BLIP3o-NEXT (baseline) | 30 | 0.91 | 82.05 | 29.42 |
| EMF (ours) | 4 | **0.90** | **81.20** | **29.25** |
| EMF (ours) | 1 | 0.74 | 77.36 | 25.77 |
| SDXL-Lightning | 4 | 0.50 | 74.24 | — |

EMF with 4 steps achieves competitive performance with the 30-step baseline while being 4.5× faster in inference time. Unlike consistency-distilled models that saturate at higher steps, EMF continues to improve with additional steps.

## Limitations & Future Work

- The approach is validated primarily on BLIP3o-NEXT; transferability to other backbones needs further investigation
- MeanFlow training remains unstable for text encoders with lower discriminability (e.g., SANA-1.5 even after encoder fine-tuning)
- Future work could explore text encoder architectures specifically designed for few-step generation
