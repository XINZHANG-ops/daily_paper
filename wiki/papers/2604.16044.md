# Elucidating the SNR-t Bias of Diffusion Probabilistic Models

**Published**: 2026-04-16 | **Added**: 2026-04-20 | **arXiv**: [2604.16044](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.16044)

## Summary

This paper identifies a fundamental bias in Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) — the Signal-to-Noise Ratio-timestep (SNR-t) bias. During training, the SNR of a sample is strictly coupled to its timestep, but during inference, cumulative prediction errors and discretization errors cause the denoising trajectory to deviate from the ideal path, resulting in misaligned SNR values. This misalignment causes error accumulation and degrades generation quality. The paper provides comprehensive empirical evidence and theoretical analysis, then proposes DCW (Differential Correction in Wavelet domain) — a training-free, plug-and-play method that decomposes samples into frequency components and applies differential correction to each component, achieving up to 47.1% FID reduction with only ~0.47% computational overhead.

## Key Contributions

- **SNR-t Bias Identification**: First comprehensive analysis of misalignment between predicted sample SNR and assigned timestep during inference
- **Theoretical Proof**: Derives analytical form of SNR in reverse process showing reverse samples always have lower SNR than forward samples at the same timestep
- **DCW (Differential Correction in Wavelet domain)**: Training-free correction leveraging denoising characteristics — DPMs reconstruct low-frequency components before high-frequency details
- **Plug-and-play**: Can be integrated with existing bias-corrected models (ADM-IP, ADM-ES, DPM-FR) for further improvements

## Method

**Key Finding 1: Network produces inaccurate predictions with mismatched SNR-t**
- Fixed timestep s, varying input sample SNR (via forward process with different t)
- Samples with lower SNR (t > s) lead to overestimated noise predictions
- Samples with higher SNR (t < s) lead to underestimated predictions

**Key Finding 2: Reverse denoising samples exhibit lower SNR than forward samples**
- For any timestep t, ||ε_θ(x̂_t, t)||_2 > ||ε_θ(x_t, t)||_2
- The denoising sample x̂_t generally maintains lower SNR than forward perturbed sample x_t

**DCW Method**:
1. At each denoising step, obtain reconstruction sample x̂_0 (directly predicts clean sample)
2. Apply DWT to decompose x̂_0 and x_{t-1} into frequency subbands (ll, lh, hl, hh)
3. Apply differential correction to each frequency component:
   - x̂_f_{t-1} = x̂_f_{t-1} + λ_f_t (x̂_f_{t-1} - x̂_0_f)
4. Apply iDWT to map back to pixel space

**Dynamic Weighting Strategy**:
- Low-frequency component: λ_l_t = λ_l · σ_t (decaying as denoising progresses)
- High-frequency component: λ_h_t = (1 - λ_h) σ_t (increasing as denoising progresses)

This aligns with the denoising characteristic: early steps focus on low-frequency contours, later steps on high-frequency details.

## Results

**CIFAR-10 Results**:

| Model | FID (T=20) | FID (T=50) |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| IDDPM Baseline | 13.19 | 5.55 |
| IDDPM + DCW | **7.57** (42.6% reduction) | **4.16** (25% reduction) |
| EDM Baseline | 10.66 | 3.74 |
| EDM + DCW | **5.67** (47.1% reduction) | **2.41** (35.6% reduction) |

**Integration with existing bias-corrected models**:
| Model | Base | + DCW |
|-------|------|-------|
| EDM-ES | 6.59 | **6.13** (7.0% reduction) |
| EDM-FR | 4.68 | **4.57** (2.4% reduction) |
| PFGM-FR | 6.62 | **6.18** (6.6% reduction) |

**Computational overhead**: ~0.47% on average across different resolutions and models.

**Qualitative improvements**: FLUX-DCW reduces artifacts like over-smoothing and overexposure compared to vanilla FLUX.
