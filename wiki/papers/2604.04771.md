# MinerU2.5-Pro: Pushing the Limits of Data-Centric Document Parsing at Scale

**Published**: 2026-04-05 | **Added**: 2026-04-07 | **arXiv**: [2604.04771](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.04771)

## Summary

MinerU2.5-Pro demonstrates that systematic data engineering—not architectural innovation—is the primary lever for advancing document parsing performance. Through cross-analysis of state-of-the-art models across diverse architectures and parameter scales, the authors identify that models exhibit highly consistent failure modes on the same hard samples, revealing that performance bottlenecks stem from shared deficiencies in training data rather than model architecture itself.

The paper introduces a Data Engine that expands training data from under 10M to 65.5M samples while improving annotation quality through three co-designed components: Diversity-and-Difficulty-Aware Sampling (DDAS) for coverage, Cross-Model Consistency Verification (CMCV) for informativeness, and Judge-and-Refine annotation pipeline for accuracy. A three-stage progressive training strategy (large-scale pre-training, hard sample fine-tuning, GRPO alignment) sequentially exploits these data quality tiers. Without any architectural modification, MinerU2.5-Pro achieves 95.69 on OmniDocBench v1.6, improving 2.71 points over the same-architecture baseline and surpassing models with over 200× more parameters.

## Key Contributions

- **Data-Centric Breakthrough**: Performance bottleneck stems from training data deficiencies, not model architecture—validates data-centric AI principles
- **Data Engine**: Three-component system co-designed for coverage, informativeness, and annotation accuracy:
  - DDAS expands data from <10M to 65.5M samples while mitigating distribution shift
  - CMCV leverages multi-model consensus to assess sample difficulty and generate reliable annotations
  - Judge-and-Refine improves hard sample annotation through render-then-verify iterative correction
- **Three-Stage Training**: Pre-training on diverse data, hard sample SFT, and GRPO format alignment
- **OmniDocBench v1.6**: Rectifies element-matching biases and introduces Hard subset for more discriminative evaluation
- **State-of-the-Art**: 95.69 on Full, 96.12 on Base, 94.08 on Hard—surpasses Gemini 3 Pro and Qwen3-VL-235B

## Method

**Data Engine Architecture**:

1. **Diversity-and-Difficulty-Aware Sampling (DDAS)**: Operates at two granularity levels
   - Page-level: Embeds pages via ViT-base, clusters via K-Means, adjusts sampling weights based on CMCV-derived difficulty within each cluster
   - Element-level: Extracts text, formula, and table blocks using layout detection, independently clusters and assesses by CMCV
   - Final sampling balances diversity (correcting long-tail shift via up/down sampling) and difficulty (upweighting Medium and Hard samples)

2. **Cross-Model Consistency Verification (CMCV)**: Uses three heterogeneous models (MinerU2.5, PaddleOCR-VL, Qwen3-VL-30B) to classify samples:
   - Easy: MinerU2.5's output consistent with at least one external model (reliable auto-annotation)
   - Medium: External models agree but MinerU2.5 differs (external consensus as pseudo-label)
   - Hard: All models exhibit significant disagreement (requires Judge-and-Refine or expert annotation)

3. **Judge-and-Refine Annotation Pipeline**: Addresses the annotation quality paradox where hard samples are most valuable but least reliably annotatable
   - Uses render-then-verify: compiles LATEX/HTML to images, feeds original + rendered images to Judge-Refine model
   - Closes cross-modal mapping gap (model excels at generating structured sequences but struggles to infer visual appearance from them)
   - Residual samples beyond automatic correction routed to targeted expert annotation

**Progressive Training Strategy**:

| Stage | Data | Scale | LR (ViT/MLP) | Batch |
|-------|------|-------|--------------|-------|
| 1: Pre-training | 65.5M CMCV auto-annotated | 65.5M | 1×10⁻⁴ / 1×10⁻³ | 256 |
| 2: Hard SFT | 192K expert-annotated + replay | 3.9M | 5×10⁻⁶ / 5×10⁻⁵ | 128 |
| 3: GRPO | High-quality subset from Stage 2 rollouts | 192K | 1×10⁻⁷ / 1×10⁻⁵ | 512 |

**OmniDocBench v1.6 Evaluation**:
- Multi-Granularity Adaptive Matching (MGAM): Corrects element-matching biases by searching optimal segmentation granularity only on prediction side
- Hard subset: 296 pages covering challenging scenarios (complex nested tables, dense formula layouts, unconventional layouts)
- Three-tier protocol: Base (1,355 pages), Hard (296 pages), Full (1,651 pages)

## Results

**OmniDocBench v1.6 Full Performance**:
- MinerU2.5-Pro: 95.69 overall
  - Text Edit: 0.036
  - Formula CDM: 97.29
  - Table TEDS: 93.42
  - Table TEDS-S: 95.92
  - Read Order: 0.120

**Comparison with Same-Architecture Baseline**:
- MinerU2.5 baseline: 92.98
- Improvement: +2.71 points (all gains attributable to data engineering)

**Hard Subset Performance** (critical differentiator):
- MinerU2.5-Pro: 94.08 (only -2.04 from Base)
- GLM-OCR: 92.01 (drops -4.18 from Base)
- PaddleOCR-VL-1.5: 92.01 (drops -3.71 from Base)
- Demonstrates strongest robustness on challenging samples

**Training Stage Ablation**:
| Configuration | Base | Hard | Full |
|---------------|------|------|------|
| Baseline | 93.23 | 91.65 | 92.98 |
| + Stage 1 (Large-Scale SFT) | 94.54 | 93.10 | 94.29 |
| + Stage 2 (Hard-Sample SFT) | 95.60 | 93.84 | 95.25 |
| + Stage 3 (GRPO) | 96.12 | 94.08 | 95.69 |

**Element-Specific Performance**:
- Text recognition: 0.019 edit distance (30.5% reduction from baseline 0.028)
- Formula recognition: CDM 97.29 on OmniDocBench Base (99.20), near performance ceiling
- Table recognition: TEDS 93.42 (4.18 improvement on Hard subset)

## Limitations & Future Work

**Evaluation Limitations**: Element-matching paradigm has inherent ambiguities—same content can have multiple equivalent notations (HTML vs Markdown for tables), and same visual layout can have different legitimate representations (human annotators may disagree).

**Coverage Gaps**: OmniDocBench v1.6 covers mainstream scenarios; vertical domains (finance, legal, medical) with higher precision requirements need domain-specific evaluation sets.

**From Parsing to Structural Understanding**: Current work focuses on content accuracy; structural relationships (hierarchical heading-body, figure-table bindings, cross-page continuity) are equally critical for retrieval and semantic understanding—advancing from "content extraction" to "structured semantic understanding" is the natural next step.
