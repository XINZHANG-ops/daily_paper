# Self-Distilled RLVR

**Published**: 2026-04-02 | **Added**: 2026-04-08 | **arXiv**: [2604.03128](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.03128)

## Summary

RLSD (Reinforcement Learning with Self-Distillation) addresses a critical failure mode in on-policy self-distillation (OPSD) for multimodal reasoning tasks. OPSD suffers from an irreducible mutual information gap between teacher and student caused by information asymmetry—the student cannot perfectly mimic the teacher because the teacher's privileged information is not available during student generation. RLSD repurposes self-distillation from distribution matching to token-level credit assignment, using the ratio of teacher-to-student log-probabilities as an importance weight while anchoring update directions to the environment reward signal.

## Key Contributions

- Identifies and formally characterizes the mutual information gap as the root cause of OPSD failure
- Repurposes self-distillation to token-level credit assignment rather than distribution matching
- Combines clipped credit assignment with a mixing coefficient schedule for stable training
- Achieves highest average accuracy (56.18%) across 5 multimodal reasoning benchmarks, 4.69% above base LLM and 2.32% above GRPO

## Method

RLSD operates in an RLVR framework where a teacher model (privileged) provides token-level guidance and the environment provides reward signals. The key innovation is separating *update direction* (determined by environment reward) from *update magnitude* (determined by privileged information gain). Token-level update magnitudes are modulated by the ratio of teacher-to-student log-probabilities—this ratio captures how "surprised" the student is relative to the teacher. Clipped credit assignment prevents overly large updates, and a mixing coefficient schedule controls the influence of self-distillation over training.

## Results

RLSD achieves the highest average accuracy across five multimodal reasoning benchmarks (56.18%), outperforming the base LLM by 4.69% and GRPO by 2.32%. It shows faster convergence and sustained improvement without the degradation observed in OPSD after extended training.

## Limitations & Future Work

RLSD still requires a teacher model with privileged information, limiting its applicability in truly self-play scenarios. The mixing coefficient schedule requires tuning. Future work could explore adaptive schedules or teacher-free alternatives.
