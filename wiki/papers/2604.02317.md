# A Simple Baseline for Streaming Video Understanding

**Published**: 2026-04-01 | **Added**: 2026-04-06 | **arXiv**: [2604.02317](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.02317)

## Summary

SIMPLESTREAM challenges the assumption that streaming video understanding requires complex memory mechanisms. The paper demonstrates that a simple sliding window of just the last 4-8 frames, fed directly to an off-the-shelf Vision Language Model (VLM), matches or exceeds complex approaches like HERMES and StreamForest that maintain memory banks, retrieval mechanisms, or compression pipelines. The key finding is a perception-memory tradeoff: adding more historical context improves recall-oriented tasks but degrades current-scene perception, because the VLM's limited context window gets diluted with non-relevant historical tokens.

## Key Contributions

- First work to show a zero-complexity sliding-window baseline matches state-of-the-art streaming methods
- Identifies and formalizes the perception-memory tradeoff in streaming video understanding
- Provides comprehensive analysis across 3 frame budgets (2, 4, 8 frames) and 2 benchmarks (OVO-Bench, StreamingBench)
- Shows memory-side gains come at a substantial perception cost—tradeoff is fundamental, not methodological

## Method

SIMPLESTREAM takes a simple approach: for each query about a video stream, extract the last N frames (N=2, 4, or 8) and feed them directly to a pretrained VLM without any additional memory mechanism, retrieval, or training. The only design choice is how many recent frames to retain. This contrasts with HERMES (memory bank), StreamForest (tree-structured memory), and Flash-VStream (KV cache compression).

## Results

With just 4 frames, SIMPLESTREAM achieves 67.7% on OVO-Bench and 80.59% on StreamingBench—surpassing HERMES and StreamForest while using the least GPU memory and maintaining competitive latency. StreamForest-7B shows the clearest memory-side gains (best recall) but pays the largest perception penalty, with errors concentrated in current-scene misclassifications.

## Limitations & Future Work

The sliding window approach is inherently limited to "backward-looking" queries; it cannot anticipate future events. Future work could explore adaptive window sizes or lightweight prediction mechanisms.
