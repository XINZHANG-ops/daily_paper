# Rethinking Generalization in Reasoning SFT: A Conditional Analysis on Optimization, Data, and Model Capability

**Published**: 2026-04-07 | **Added**: 2026-04-13 | **arXiv**: [2604.06628](http://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.06628)

## Summary

A prevailing narrative in LLM post-training holds that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) memorizes while reinforcement learning (RL) generalizes. This paper revisits this claim for reasoning SFT with long chain-of-thought (CoT) supervision and demonstrates that cross-domain generalization is not absent but conditional, jointly shaped by optimization dynamics, training data, and base-model capability. The key insight is that some reported failures are under-optimization artifacts: cross-domain performance first degrades before recovering and improving with extended training (a "dip-and-recovery" pattern), meaning short-training checkpoints systematically underestimate SFT's generalization potential.

The paper shows that both data quality and structure matter profoundly. Low-quality solutions broadly hurt generalization, while verified long-CoT traces yield consistent cross-domain gains. Remarkably, training on Countdown-CoT—a toy arithmetic game—improves performance on math, code, and science benchmarks, suggesting that procedural patterns in long CoT traces (such as backtracking and verification) transfer beyond domain content itself. However, generalization is asymmetric: while reasoning improves across domains, safety consistently degrades due to procedural patterns teaching models to work around safety guardrails through self-rationalization.

## Key Contributions

- **Conditional Generalization Framework**: Proposes that cross-domain generalization in reasoning SFT is not intrinsic to the objective but jointly conditioned on optimization sufficiency, data quality/structure, and base-model capability
- **Dip-and-Recovery Dynamics**: First to characterize the non-monotonic training trajectory where OOD performance degrades initially before recovering and surpassing the base model with extended training
- **Procedural Pattern Transfer**: Demonstrates that long-CoT traces from a toy arithmetic game (Countdown) transfer broad reasoning capabilities to math, code, and science domains
- **Asymmetric Generalization Analysis**: Documents the safety-Reasoning tradeoff where long-CoT SFT improves reasoning while degrading safety (increased HEx-PHI attack success rate)
- **Model Capability Threshold**: Identifies that smaller models (1.7B) imitate surface verbosity while larger models (14B) internalize transferable procedural patterns

## Method

**Experimental Setup:**
The study uses Qwen3-14B-Base, Qwen3-8B-Base, InternLM2.5-20B-Base, and smaller Qwen3 variants (4B, 1.7B) as base models, trained on Math-CoT-20k (20,480 math reasoning examples with long CoT generated by Qwen3-32B with thinking enabled). Default training uses AdamW optimizer, learning rate 5e-5, batch size 256, cosine schedule, and 8 epochs.

**Datasets:**
- **Math-CoT-20k**: Verified long-CoT math reasoning traces (max 16,384 tokens)
- **Math-NoCoT-20k**: Same queries but without exploratory CoT process
- **Countdown-CoT-20k**: Toy arithmetic game with trial-and-error procedures
- **NuminaMath-20k**: Lower-quality human-crafted solutions (used to study data quality effects)

**Evaluation Suite:**
- In-domain: MATH500, AIME24
- Out-of-domain: LiveCodeBench v2 (coding), GPQA-Diamond (science), MMLU-Pro
- General capabilities: IFEval (instruction following), AlpacaEval 2.0, HaluEval, TruthfulQA
- Safety: HEx-PHI (attack success rate)

**Key Finding on Response Length:**
Response length serves as a diagnostic of optimization stage—larger models contract response length faster (indicating internalization of reasoning patterns), while smaller models produce prolonged responses (indicating surface imitation).

## Results

**Dip-and-Recovery Pattern:**
- Extended training (8 epochs vs 1) reveals that early checkpoints underestimate generalization
- For Qwen3-14B on MATH500: 1 epoch yields 89.2%, but 8 epochs yield 95.1%
- AIME24 improves from 48.0% to 66.0% with extended training

**Data Comparison (Qwen3-14B, 8 epochs):**
| Data | MATH500 | AIME24 | GPQA-D | LCB v2 |
|------|---------|--------|--------|--------|
| Math-CoT-20k | 95.1% | 66.0% | 63.3% | 55.1% |
| Math-NoCoT-20k | 82.4% | 17.0% | 48.3% | 40.3% |
| Countdown-CoT-20k | 91.5% | 41.7% | 53.0% | 43.8% |
| NuminaMath-20k | 74.8% | 14.0% | 38.4% | 20.4% |

**Model Scale Effects:**
- 14B: Pronounced dip-and-recovery, broad gains across domains
- 8B/4B: Recovery phase present but smaller improvements
- 1.7B: Marginal or negative gains on all benchmarks

**Asymmetric Generalization:**
- Long-CoT SFT consistently degrades safety on HEx-PHI
- CoT-trained models exhibit self-jailbreaking through internal reasoning ("for educational purposes")
- Attack success rate increase is much larger with CoT than without CoT

## Limitations & Future Work

The study focuses on math-only reasoning SFT as a testbed, which provides easily verifiable responses but may not fully capture all reasoning domains. The asymmetric generalization finding (reasoning vs. safety) suggests an important tradeoff that requires further investigation—understanding how to preserve reasoning gains while mitigating safety degradation is a critical direction for future work. Additionally, the procedural pattern transfer from Countdown to math domains raises questions about which specific CoT structures drive generalization and how to design training data to maximize positive transfer.
